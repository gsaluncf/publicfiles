The Problem: Four Scenarios

Understanding the RMD vs Step-Up tension through concrete examples

The Setup

Meet Robert, age 65, who has just retired with:

  • $1,000,000 in a Traditional IRA (tax-deferred; all withdrawals taxed as income)
  • $500,000 in a taxable brokerage account (cost basis: $200,000, unrealized gain: $300,000)

Robert is in the 24% federal tax bracket. Capital gains tax is 15%. He wants to maximize what he leaves to his daughter.

The Two Rules in Conflict

Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) Step-Up in Basis

What: Starting at age 73, the IRS forces Robert to withdraw a minimum amount each year from his Traditional IRA.

How much: Account balance divided by a life expectancy factor. At 73, the factor is 26.5. So $1,000,000 / 26.5 = $37,736/year minimum.

Tax impact: Each withdrawal is taxed as ordinary income. At 24%, that's $9,057 in taxes on the first RMD alone.

What: When Robert dies, his daughter inherits his taxable brokerage account at its current market value, not what Robert paid.

Example: Robert bought stock for $200,000. It's now worth $500,000. If Robert sells, he pays 15% on $300,000 gain = $45,000 in taxes.

If Robert dies first: Daughter inherits at $500,000 basis. She sells immediately. Gain = $0. Taxes = $0.

The Tension

RMDs force money out of tax-advantaged accounts and into taxable income.

Step-Up rewards waiting by eliminating capital gains taxes at death.

The optimal strategy depends on how long Robert lives and what the market does. Let's see how this plays out.

Scenario 1: Early Death (Age 70)

Robert dies at 70, before RMDs begin. Assume the market returned 5% annually.

Asset Value at Death Daughter's Tax Net to Daughter
Traditional IRA $1,276,282 $306,308 (24%) $969,974
Taxable Brokerage $638,141 $0 (step-up!) $638,141
TOTAL $1,914,423 $306,308 $1,608,115

Key insight: The $438,141 in unrealized gains ($638,141 - $200,000) was completely erased by the step-up. That's $65,721 in taxes avoided (15% of $438,141).

Scenario 2: Long Life (Age 90)

Robert lives to 90. He takes RMDs from age 73-90 (17 years). Same 5% annual return.

Item Amount
Total RMDs taken (ages 73-90) $987,432
Taxes paid on RMDs (24%) $236,984
Remaining IRA at death $1,847,293
Taxable brokerage at death $1,723,879

Now daughter inherits a larger estate, but Robert paid $236,984 in RMD taxes over 17 years. The step-up still applies to the brokerage account, but its relative benefit is smaller compared to the RMD drag.

The Question Emerges

In Scenario 1, holding everything worked well. In Scenario 2, the RMD taxes accumulated significantly. At what lifespan does the strategy flip?

Scenario 3: Bad Market Timing

Robert dies at 80, but the market crashed 40% in year 6 and never fully recovered. Average return over 15 years: 2%.

Item Good Market (5%) Bad Market (2%)
IRA at death $1,578,947 $1,104,622
Brokerage at death $1,039,464 $672,406
RMD taxes paid $142,105 $98,432
Net to daughter $2,097,259 $1,438,923

Key insight: Market returns matter enormously. The same lifespan produces a $658,336 difference in outcomes. And notice: in the bad market, the step-up benefit is smaller because there's less unrealized gain to erase.

Scenario 4: The Alternative Strategy

What if Robert had been more aggressive? Instead of just taking RMDs, he converts $100,000/year from Traditional IRA to Roth IRA starting at age 65.

Strategy Description Tax Timing
Hold to Death Take only required RMDs. Keep brokerage untouched. Pay taxes later (RMDs) or never (step-up)
Aggressive Conversion Convert IRA to Roth early. Pay taxes now at known rates. Pay taxes now, grow tax-free forever

If Robert converts $100,000/year for 8 years (ages 65-72), he pays $192,000 in taxes upfront (24%). But:

  • His RMDs starting at 73 are much smaller (less in Traditional IRA)
  • The Roth grows tax-free and passes to daughter tax-free
  • He "locked in" the 24% rate before potential future tax increases

Which Strategy Wins?

It depends on:

  • How long Robert lives - Longer life favors early conversion (more time for Roth to grow)
  • Market returns - Higher returns favor early conversion (more tax-free growth)
  • Future tax rates - If rates rise, early conversion wins

We cannot calculate a single answer. We need to simulate thousands of scenarios and see which strategy wins more often.

Why This Requires Simulation

We have shown four scenarios with specific assumptions. But reality has:

  • Uncertain lifespan - Robert could die at 68 or 98. We don't know.
  • Uncertain returns - The market could return -20% or +30% in any year.
  • Path dependence - A crash at age 74 is worse than a crash at age 85 (sequence of returns risk).

A single calculation cannot capture this. We need to:

  1. Generate thousands of possible market paths
  2. Generate thousands of possible lifespans
  3. Apply each strategy to each (market, lifespan) pair
  4. Compare the distribution of outcomes
