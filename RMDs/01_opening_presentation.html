Session 1: The Problem

Understanding wealth management data through a real tax optimization problem

What Is Wealth Management?

Wealth management is a service business. Clients pay advisors to help them make financial decisions: how to invest, when to sell, how to minimize taxes, how to plan for retirement, how to pass money to their kids.

Behind every recommendation is data. Lots of it:

  • Holdings data: What does the client own? Stocks, bonds, real estate, cash.
  • Tax lot data: When did they buy each asset? At what price? This determines taxes when they sell.
  • Account data: Is the money in a 401(k)? An IRA? A regular brokerage account? Each has different tax rules.
  • Market data: What are current prices? What are expected returns?
  • Client data: How old are they? What is their risk tolerance? When do they need the money?

This seminar uses a specific wealth management problem to teach you how this data works. You will not become a financial advisor. But you will understand how to model financial data and make decisions under uncertainty.

The Three Types of Investment Accounts

In the United States, there are three main types of accounts where people hold investments. Each has different tax treatment:

Account Type When You Put Money In While It Grows When You Take Money Out
Traditional IRA / 401(k) Tax deduction (you pay less tax now) No taxes on growth Taxed as ordinary income
Roth IRA / Roth 401(k) No deduction (you already paid tax) No taxes on growth Tax-free
Taxable Brokerage No deduction Dividends taxed each year Capital gains tax on profits

Most retirees have money in multiple account types. The question is: which account should they take money from first?

What Is a Tax Lot?

When you buy shares of a stock, you create a "tax lot." A tax lot records:

  • What you bought (e.g., 100 shares of Apple)
  • When you bought it (e.g., March 15, 2020)
  • What you paid (e.g., $250 per share, so $25,000 total)

The price you paid is called your cost basis. When you sell, you pay taxes on the difference between the sale price and your cost basis. This difference is your capital gain.

Example: You bought 100 shares of Apple at $250 (cost basis = $25,000). Today Apple is $400 per share (value = $40,000). If you sell, your capital gain is $15,000. You owe taxes on that $15,000.

But if you never sell, you never owe taxes. The gain is "unrealized" - it exists on paper but you have not locked it in.

Two Tax Rules That Create Our Problem

The U.S. tax code has two rules that pull in opposite directions. Understanding these rules is the key to understanding our problem.

Rule 1: Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

The government gave you a tax break when you put money into your Traditional IRA or 401(k). But they want their taxes eventually. So starting at age 73, the IRS forces you to withdraw money from these accounts every year.

The amount you must withdraw is based on your account balance and your age. At 73, you must withdraw about 3.8% of your balance. At 80, about 5%. At 90, about 8.8%. The percentage increases as you age because the IRS assumes you have fewer years left.

Every dollar you withdraw is taxed as ordinary income. If you are in the 24% tax bracket, you lose 24 cents of every dollar to taxes.

The IRS is saying: "We let you defer taxes for decades. Now pay up."

Rule 2: Step-Up in Basis

When you die, something remarkable happens to your taxable brokerage account. Your heirs inherit your assets at their current market value, not at your original cost basis.

Remember that Apple stock you bought at $250 that is now worth $400? If you sell it, you owe taxes on the $150 gain. But if you die holding it, your daughter inherits it with a cost basis of $400. If she sells it immediately for $400, her gain is zero. She owes nothing.

All those years of appreciation? The IRS forgives them. The unrealized gains disappear.

The IRS is saying: "If you hold until death, we will pretend the gains never happened."

The Conflict

These two rules create a tension:

  • RMDs force you to take money out of tax-deferred accounts and pay taxes now.
  • Step-up rewards you for holding appreciated assets in taxable accounts until death.

If you knew exactly when you would die and exactly what the market would do, you could calculate the optimal strategy. But you do not know either of those things.

Meet Robert: A Concrete Example

Robert is 65 years old. He just retired. He has:

  • $1,000,000 in a Traditional IRA. He contributed over 30 years of working. He got tax deductions on every contribution. He has never paid taxes on any of this money.
  • $500,000 in a taxable brokerage account. He invested $200,000 over the years. It has grown to $500,000. He has $300,000 in unrealized gains.

Robert needs $60,000 per year to live on. He has two children he wants to leave money to.

Robert has a choice:

Strategy A: Spend Down the IRA First

Robert takes $60,000 per year from his IRA. He pays income tax on every withdrawal (let's say 22%, so $13,200 per year in taxes). His taxable brokerage account continues to grow untouched.

When Robert dies, his children inherit the brokerage account with a stepped-up basis. They owe no taxes on the $300,000+ in gains.

Strategy B: Spend the Taxable Account First

Robert takes $60,000 per year from his taxable brokerage account. He pays capital gains tax on the gains portion (let's say 15%, so less than the income tax rate). His IRA continues to grow untouched.

But at age 73, RMDs kick in. Robert is forced to withdraw from his IRA whether he needs the money or not. And his IRA has been growing for 8 more years, so the RMDs are larger. He pays income tax on all of it.

Which Strategy Wins?

It depends on two things Robert cannot control:

  1. How long Robert lives. If he dies at 70, he never faces RMDs. Strategy B wins because his children get the step-up on the brokerage account AND inherit the IRA (which they can stretch over 10 years). If he lives to 95, he faces 22 years of RMDs that drain his IRA anyway.
  2. What the market does. If the market crashes, his unrealized gains shrink and the step-up is worth less. If the market booms, his IRA grows larger and RMDs become more painful.

This Is Our Problem

We cannot calculate the answer because we do not know the inputs. We need to simulate thousands of possible futures - different lifespans, different market returns - and see which strategy wins more often, and by how much.

This is what you will build in this seminar.

What You Will Build

Over the next 1.5 days, you will build a simulation that answers Robert's question. The simulation will:

  1. Generate 10,000 possible market futures using a model called Geometric Brownian Motion (we will explain this)
  2. Generate 10,000 possible lifespans using real actuarial data from the Social Security Administration
  3. Apply the tax rules to each combination of market path and lifespan
  4. Store the results in a database so you can query them
  5. Find the answer: Under what conditions does Strategy A beat Strategy B? By how much? How confident can we be?

You will use Python for the simulation and DuckDB for the database. By the end, you will have a working system that can answer questions like: "If Robert has a 60% chance of living past 85, which strategy maximizes expected wealth for his children?"
