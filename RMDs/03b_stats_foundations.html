Statistics Foundations: Why Averages Fail

Building intuition before we build code

The Dangerous Statement

"The stock market returns 7% per year on average."

This statement is technically true and practically useless for retirement planning. This page explains why, and what we need instead.

Problem 1: Volatility Destroys Averages

Consider two investment paths over 4 years, both with the same arithmetic average return of 5%:

Year Path A (Steady) Path B (Volatile)
Year 1 +5% +30%
Year 2 +5% -20%
Year 3 +5% +25%
Year 4 +5% -15%
Average Return 5% 5%

Starting with $100,000:

Metric Path A Path B
Final Value $121,551 $110,500
Actual Compound Return 5.0% per year 2.5% per year

The Lesson

Volatility is a drag on compound returns. Path B had the same average return but ended with $11,051 less. This is called "volatility drag" and it's why we cannot use simple averages for financial planning.

Problem 2: Sequence of Returns Risk

The order of returns matters enormously when you're withdrawing money. Consider two retirees who both experience the same returns, just in different order:

Year Retiree A Retiree B Withdrawal
Year 1 +15% -15% $50,000
Year 2 +10% -10% $50,000
Year 3 -10% +10% $50,000
Year 4 -15% +15% $50,000

Both retirees start with $1,000,000 and experience the exact same set of returns. Only the order differs.

Outcome Retiree A (good start) Retiree B (bad start)
Portfolio after 4 years $823,687 $659,045

Same returns. Different order. $164,642 difference.

Why This Matters for RMDs

RMDs force withdrawals every year. A market crash early in retirement is devastating because you're selling shares at low prices. This is exactly the kind of path-dependent risk that averages cannot capture.

Problem 3: Lifespan is Unknown

We showed in the Scenarios page that dying at 70 vs 90 completely changes which strategy wins. But we don't know when someone will die.

Here's what actuarial data tells us about a 65-year-old male:

Age at Death Probability Cumulative
Before 70 8% 8%
70-75 12% 20%
75-80 18% 38%
80-85 22% 60%
85-90 20% 80%
90+ 20% 100%

Planning for the "average" lifespan of 82 ignores that 40% of people will live longer and 60% will die sooner.

The Solution: Simulation

Since we cannot predict the future, we simulate many possible futures and analyze the distribution of outcomes.

The Monte Carlo Approach

  1. Generate random market paths - Not one path, but 10,000 different possible market histories
  2. Generate random lifespans - Sample from actuarial tables to get 10,000 possible death ages
  3. Pair them up - Each simulation combines one market path with one lifespan
  4. Apply the strategy - Calculate taxes, RMDs, and terminal wealth for each simulation
  5. Analyze the distribution - What's the average outcome? The worst 5%? How often does Strategy A beat Strategy B?

What We Need to Model

Uncertainty Statistical Model Key Parameters
Market returns Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM) Drift (expected return), Volatility (standard deviation)
Lifespan Survival Analysis Actuarial life tables (age-specific mortality rates)

Preview: The Questions We Can Answer

Once we build the simulation, we can answer questions like:

  • Expected Value: "On average, how much more wealth does Strategy A generate?"
  • Risk: "What's the worst outcome in the bottom 5% of simulations?"
  • Win Rate: "In what percentage of simulations does Strategy A beat Strategy B?"
  • Decision Boundary: "At what volatility level does the optimal strategy change?"

Concept Why It Matters
